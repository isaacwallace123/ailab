# Compare Financial Scenarios

1. Clarify jurisdiction, currency, household goal, time horizon, cash-flow constraints, emergency reserve, debts, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and whether amounts are before or after tax.
2. Define a baseline and at least two alternatives. Keep contributions and timing comparable; identify any option that changes risk, liquidity, guarantees, insurance, or tax treatment.
3. Use deterministic finance tools for supported arithmetic. Do not perform compound-growth, loan, inflation, or retirement projection arithmetic mentally when a tool is available.
4. Show nominal and inflation-adjusted results, contributions, fees, taxes if reliably known, debt interest avoided, and ending balances. Keep uncertain returns separate from contractual rates.
5. Use conservative, base, and optimistic assumptions—not a single forecast. Include at least one adverse sequence or lower-return case for retirement or investment comparisons.
6. Do not hardcode current contribution limits, tax brackets, benefits, or product rules. Verify them from the relevant government or regulator and date the figures.
7. Explain break-even points, downside risk, opportunity cost, liquidity, penalties, and what assumptions drive the ranking. A higher projected balance is not automatically the safer choice.
8. Present a compact table and a plain-language conclusion, then list the inputs to verify with a licensed adviser, tax professional, lender, or plan administrator where appropriate.
